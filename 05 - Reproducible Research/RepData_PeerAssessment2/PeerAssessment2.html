Desciprtion

Your data analysis must address the following questions:

  • Across the United States, which types of events (as indicated in the EVTYPE variable) are most harmful with respect to population health?
  • Across the United States, which types of events have the greatest economic consequences?

Consider writing your report as if it were to be read by a government or municipal manager who might be responsible for preparing for severe weather events and will need to prioritize resources for different types of events. However, there is no need to make any specific recommendations in your report.

Synopsys

In this report, I will investigate the most dangerous environmental disasters, based on the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) database, which was collected between 1950 and 2011.

Using simple bar charts, I will show which type of disasters cost most lives, cause most injuries and cause the most damage to properties and crops.

Finally, let’s check what kind of EVTYPEs are there in the data.

length(unique(data$EVTYPE))
## [1] 985

That is almost a 1000 diffent type of events. Some of which are similar but different subcategories for example thunderstorms with different wind speeds or hails with different ice sizes. Some of them are really similar, e.g.: heat wave, heat waves, excessive heat, extreme heat. There are even typos (“thundeerstorm”, “tunderstorm”) and mislabeled data (“Summary of May 22 am”). Also, there are some categories that are the same but with different letter case (e.g.: “wind” and “WIND”).

# upper case everything
data$EVTYPE <- toupper(data$EVTYPE)
# trim leading whitespace
data$EVTYPE <- str_trim(data$EVTYPE)
# replace some common subcategories / errors using grepl
data$EVTYPE[grepl("THUNDERSTORM", data$EVTYPE)] <- "THUNDERSTORM"
data$EVTYPE[grepl("THUNDEERSTORM", data$EVTYPE)] <- "THUNDERSTORM"
data$EVTYPE[grepl("TUNDERSTORM", data$EVTYPE)] <- "THUNDERSTORM"

Preprocessing the data

There are two column, PROPDMGEXP and CROPDMGEXP, that contains the exponents but not in numeric format (instead, in text i.e.: B for billion). Convert it to numeric format and save it to a variable with a SUM suffix (using a new variable is convenient since we can fill it up with zeros and not worry about NAs)

data$PROPDMGEXPNUM <- rep(0, nrow(data))
data$PROPDMGEXPNUM[data$PROPDMGEXP == "B"] <- 9
data$PROPDMGEXPNUM[data$PROPDMGEXP == "M"] <- 6
data$PROPDMGEXPNUM[data$PROPDMGEXP == "K"] <- 3
data$PROPDMGEXPNUM[data$PROPDMGEXP == "H"] <- 2
data$CROPDMGEXPNUM <- rep(0, nrow(data))
data$CROPDMGEXPNUM[data$CROPDMGEXP == "B"] <- 9
data$CROPDMGEXPNUM[data$CROPDMGEXP == "M"] <- 6
data$CROPDMGEXPNUM[data$CROPDMGEXP == "K"] <- 3
data$CROPDMGEXPNUM[data$CROPDMGEXP == "H"] <- 2

And since we’re here, let’s calculate the actual damage values.

data$actualPropDmg <- data$PROPDMG * 10 ^ data$PROPDMGEXPNUM
data$actualCropDmg <- data$CROPDMG * 10 ^ data$CROPDMGEXPNUM

Also convert the dates from string to actual date variables

data$BGN_DATE <- strptime(data$BGN_DATE, "%m/%d/%Y %H:%M:%S")
data$END_DATE <- strptime(data$END_DATE, "%m/%d/%Y %H:%M:%S")

Most costful events

We’ll do the same as before but now for cost of property and crop damage.

# save to overall for later use

colnames(prop) <- c("EvType", "Property_damage")
colnames(overall) <- c("EvType", "Damage")

crop <- aggregate(data$actualCropDmg, by = list(data$EVTYPE), "sum")

Results

A short research showed us which environmental disasters are the most costful. In terms of injuries, tornadoes are the worst by far. In terms of property or crop damage, floods are the worst, followed by hurricanes and tornadoes. However, only in terms of crop damage, droughts are worse than floods.
